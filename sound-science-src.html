Introduction

The streaming platform, Spotify, provides valuable data that allow us to explore how song attributes like tempo, energy, and danceability influence streaming success. By comparing these characteristics across songs with different streaming rates, we aim to uncover patterns that can offer insights into what drives listener engagement. This analysis can help artists, producers, and record labels make more informed decisions to increase their chances of success in a competitive market.

The primary questions we aim to answer are: What strategies can music producers employ to boost streaming rates? What factors should record labels consider when selecting songs for release? What elements contribute to a song’s lower popularity?

Our datasets allow us to identify common elements across songs for comparison. We are analyzing various attributes, including danceability, tempo, loudness, energy, valence, and acoustic characteristics, to uncover patterns and generate meaningful insights.

Most streamed songs of all time (Spotify): https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/meeratif/spotify-most-streamed-songs-of-all-time This dataset, collected by Meeratif from Pakistan and recently updated, was sourced from Spotify’s public API. It includes the platform’s most-streamed songs through 2022-23, with detailed statistics for each track, including the artist, title, total streams, and daily stream counts.

Spotify Music Characteristics Dataset: https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/vatsalmavani/spotify-dataset The data appears to have been generated using Spotify’s API or made publicly available by Spotify, possibly through their 2020 partnership with AICrowd to release the Million Playlists Dataset (MPD). However, this is not explicitly stated.

An ideas to ponder about would be “If it is fair for Spotify to use data like play counts (streaming rates) to decide which songs and artists get promoted?” A bias that existed were the same song titles featured in both datasets, but with either a solo artists or a version with a featured artists included. This could lead to a slight incorrect data comparison, which has been taken into consideration.

Data Documentation

  • How was the data in the Spotify datasets collected?

    • The Spotify Music Characteristics Dataset includes attributes like tempo, loudness, and danceability. However, the data source does not specify the collection methods, timeframe, or data origin, limiting insights into the reliability and representativeness of the data.

    • The Spotify Most Streamed Songs Dataset aggregates information on the most-streamed songs on Spotify, including the number of streams and artist information. There’s no mention of data collection methods, potentially missing insights about data selection criteria (e.g., region or time period).

  • What information do the dataset columns provide, and how were they cleaned?

    • The Spotify Music Characteristics Dataset includes columns such as tempo, danceability, and loudness. These are likely derived from Spotify’s audio analysis tools, but no official codebook was found to confirm exact definitions. Since official definitions are unavailable, we interpreted these as measures reflecting the energy, volume, and rhythm suitability for dancing.

    • In the Spotify Most Streamed Songs Dataset, the Artist.and.Title column was split into artist and name. The Streams column required cleaning to remove non-numeric characters and convert values to numeric for analysis (see code below).

  • What are the potential sources of missing data, and how were they addressed?

    • The potential source of missing data revolves around one dataset including featured artists in the track, while the other dataset only consists of the main artist. This was addressed by cleaning the song name columns within both datasets and joining by song title––without factoring in artists.

  • What biases or limitations might exist in the dataset?

    • A bias that existed were the same song titles featured in both datasets, but with either a solo artists or a version with a featured artists included. This could lead to a slight incorrect data comparison. We have not been able to find a solution to this thus far, but hope to implement a solution that does take this into account in the future.

In short, although slower music tends to result in more streams overall, its effects are less convincing when one fixates on any one category individually due to high variability and high standard error values.

To end on an optimistic note, while there are some things you can do as a music producer to increase streams in a purely objective sense (loudness, tempo), the correlations are mild at best, and get reduced and degraded very rapidly as you fixate on popularity subgroups, and take into consideration things like Spotify’s loudness normalization. Put concisely, creativity, and uniqueness are still the most important elements of music production. They transcend any one metric, data point, or correlation. If you’re happy with your work, and you believe those two pillars have been addressed, only then might it make sense to increase the final mix volume by a couple decibels, or decrease the song’s tempo.

What factors should record labels consider when selecting songs for release?

Record labels when choosing what songs to release is crucial for marketing the music that resonates with listeners. By analyzing key factors from the data we can identify which attributes are usually associated with higher streaming rates and it’ll show us what characteristics record labels should look for.

The first factor is danceability. As this reflects how well a track is made for dancing, based on its tempo and rhythm. Popular tracks tend to have higher danceability scores.

With this analysis we can see that songs with a high danceability rating generally attract more streams. Therefore record labels should prioritize tracks with higher danceability scores.

The next thing record labels should look at when releasing music is the Energy in a song. As energy measures the intensity and activity level of a song. It was observed that high-energy songs often gain more streams.

Another factor record labels should look at when releasing music is whether or not its explicit or not.
